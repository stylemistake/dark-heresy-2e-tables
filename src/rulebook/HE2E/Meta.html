Welcome to the grim, dark future
In the world of Warhammer, there is only war. The once-great Imperium of man is on the brink of collapse. Every day, it must face xenos, traitors, heretics and demons, everywhere from the battlefields in space to the back yards of withering noble estates. Humans are struggling to cope with the emergance of rare witches and mutants. AI is banned, and intricacies of technology have become so forgotten, that it is more often a subject of worship than study. Havens of peace are rare and far inbetween. Prepare to sacrifice all while fighting an insignificant fight, trying to delay the inevitable collapse of the human empire and the demise of God Emperor, who watches over us.		Philosophy: balance between roleplay and rules
The core philosophy of HE2E is that there is no fun without real challenge. Both automatic success (too easy) and constant undeserved kicks in the balls (too hard) makes for a dramatic story, but also leaves small room for roleplaying, and results in a terrible game. DH is not about that. An ideal DH campaign presents the players with a variety of challenges that require different skillsets and specialists - leading to all characters having both their moment of glory, and their downtime.
These rules aim to simulate and standardize understandable and fair obstacles when players try to complete their objective. Roleplaying, then, is what emerges when characters face these obstacles, and shines in the way they choose to overcome them. It should be noted that role-playing alone does not guarantee success, and it is possible to make objectively bad choices.

What is Holy Excel, 2nd edition
These sheets describe the rules to play a role-playing game set in the Warhammer universe. Most of the rules are heavily inspired and based on Dark Heresy 1st edition rulebook, with some additions from Rogue Trader and 2nd edition. Therefore, it is best if the players assume the roles of the Inquisition or Rogue Traders. HE2 aims to provide a finely-tuned single platform for different content found in the rulebooks, and to allow for a challenging and deep game with meaningful choices. It integrates various content, and balances it.

Distinguishing between the player and the character
Having said that rules are important, this is also a role-playing game. Players should keep in mind that they are controlling characters with their own personality, history, and interests. Some are smart, some ar ignorant, and some are straigh foolhardy. While this is reflected in characteristics to some degree, it is almost impossible to hard-code character personalities into the rules.
Therefore, players are strongly encouraged to define the personality of their character, and to roleplay it consistently and adequately. This means that the actions of a character should depend more on the character itself, rather than on the social or intelectual skills of the player. Players may want to occasionaly think "what would the character do" from time to time. Also, quieter or more reserved players that have social characters should not be punished for talking less. Keeping the focus on the characters is ultimately the job of the GM, but it is imperative that this concept is also understood by the players.

Where to start?
If you are joining an experienced group, almost no preparation is necessary. Read the principles described on the right of this page, contact your GM, ask for his help to create your character, and come to the next game session. The rules are designed to be common sence - with some demons and sci-fi involved. It should be easy enough to follow your first sessions just by intuition.
If everyone in your group is new, you're screwed. We recommend reading DH:Core rulebook first.

VARIATIONS OF DICE
ROUNDING UP AND MULTIPLYING
50
There are 3 types of rolls that you will encounter most often when playing HE2E:
d10: roll a 10-sided dye. Unless stated otherwise, 0 counts as 10 and is the highest result.
d5: roll a 10-sided dye and divide the result by two, rounding up. 0 counts as 5, and is the highest result.
d100 roll: roll two different 10-sided dye, one standing for the 10's, and the other for 1's.

Sometimes, combinations of these rolls will be used. For example, 3d10-2 means that you should roll 3 10-sided dice, and deduct 2 from the result.		If a fraction is generated when dividing, unless speciﬁed otherwise, round the result up, even if the fraction is less than one-half.

When two or more multipliers apply to a value, add them together instead of multiplying them. Each multiplier beyond the ﬁrst adds 1 less than its value to the ﬁrst multiplier. Thus a double (x2) and a double (x2) applied to the same number results in a triple (x3, as 2 + 1 = 3). A triple (x3) and a quadruple (x4) multipliers would result in a x6 modifier (3 + (4-1) = 6)

TESTS IN GENERAL
DEGREES OF SUCCESS AND FAILURE
60
A "Test" is a comparison between a result of a roll (usually d100), and one of the characters characteristics. If, after adding up all modifiers and conditions, the character's characteristic is higher or equal to the roll - he succeeds. If the characteristic is lower, he fails.

Keep in mind that there are other d100 rolls that are not tests. An example of this would be a roll to pick a result, such as a roll on the psychic phenomena table.		Whenever you roll on a Test, you either succeed or fail. However, the degree to which you do this differs. A single degree is equal to exactly 10 points deviation from the threshold.
You get 1 DoS (success) for each 10 points that your roll is lower than the threshold.
You get 1 DoF (failure) for each 10 points that your roll is higher than the threshold.

MODIFIERS
66
VARIATIONS OF TESTS
Various conditions may make the test easier or harder, increasing or lowering the threshold needed to pass the tests. Hindering conditions subtract your characteristic, making it harder to roll lower than it is. Helpful conditions add to the characteristic, making it easier to roll lower.

Unless stated otherwise, after adding up all of the modifiers, the total modifier must be between -60 and +60.
67
There are several types of tests:
Standard test is always taken, unless stated otherwise. Simply roll 1d100, add the modifiers, and compare it to the character's characteristic. The character instantly succeeds or fails.
Opposed test is usually (but not always) taken when directly competing with another character. It should always be clear which side actively initiates the test. Initiator is the side which has or had the intent to make the action that caused the test. The initiator has the disadvantage.
Both sides should roll a Standart test on relevant characteristics. The side who gains more Degrees of Success, wins. If both parties have equal DoS, then the side with the higher characteristic bonus wins. If both characteristic bonuses are equal, then the lower dice roll wins.
If both parties fail their test, then the initiator loses. A grappled victim would struggle in wain, and a sneaking assassin would make so much noise than it would be hard not to notice it. In some situations, GM may instead ask both sides to reroll, if he feels that this would be more appropriate.
Extended test takes much longer than a standard test, but is the same in principle.

REPEATING, REROLLING, AND DIFFICULTY OF TESTS
73
Tests should be required when in a challenging situation, and when failure would have consequences. In short, a test must be meaningful. Asking for many tests will reduce their individual impact, and will slow down the gameplay.
It is imperative to understand that repeatedly asking for the same test or allowing rerolls dramatically changes the maths and balance of the game. To illustrate, imagine a simple tech-priest attempting to activate a long-asleep machine spirit (Challenging +0 test, 40 intelligence, no bonuses). In his attempt, the tech-priest has a 40% chance to succeed. Now, let's say that the GM determines that this test takes a minute, and the tech-priest can continue his efforts indefinitely if he fails. After 6 attempts, the total chance to succeed would be 95%, which makes chances of failure insignificant.
Thus, all that multiple rolls achieve, is that it makes the player pointlessly roll the dice. If the GM intends to make the test only slightly harder or easier, a flat boost or penalty to the first test should be chosen at all times, with minimal chances for a reroll.
