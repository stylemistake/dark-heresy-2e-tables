TERMINOLOGY
36
Availability: the relative accessibility and frequency of the item. Higher availability makes items more available. Availability modifier varies from +40 (very frequent) to -60 (unique).
Location penalty: items are harder to access in remote locations. A trading center or a generic supply node provides no penalties. Locations away from trading centers, such as slums or small villages, gain at least a -10 modifier. Hardly accessible locations, such as deep caves or uninhabited planets, provide at least a -30 modifier.
Faction bonus: some items are rare outside a specific faction. This is represented in the general availability of the item. If the item is requested when having access to a major headquarters / trade node that belongs to the relevant faction, the availability increases by 2 steps.
Pushing contacts: Represents how many resources you commit to getting the item. Choose between no modifiers, small push, major push, and all in. (see table above)
Openly flash badge: Represents revealing your inquisitorial status. This is always active when overly dealing in Inquisition-controlled headquarters or major starships. See the table above.

TAKING THE TESTSUCCESS
43
When you want to requisition an item, make a test and compare it to your current Influence. If you roll equal or less, you pass.
The test is modified by:
• The item's base availability;
• Location penalty and the faction bonus, if there is any;
• How much you push your contacts, if you do;
• Whether you display your Inquisitorial affiliation (requires proof at GM's discretion)
• Other factors that the GM deems significant.

Immediately after taking the test, regardless of the result, reduce your influence depending on the rarity of the item you requested, how many contacts you pushed, and other criteria. This counts as your default Influence loss, which may increase if you significantly fail the test.
If your Influence goes below zero, deduct the rest directly from your Status. Then, proceed to the results - whether of success, or of failure.		You and your contacts were able to locate the item. You can claim it in the current locale after some time has passed (see the table above). If the item is unmovable (such as a service in a building), this is how long it will take to get there (with shorter durations meaning that contacts also provide you with transportation, and longer - meaning that you have to walk, ect.).
Weapons come with a free holster. Weapons with ammo come with 1 free clip (loaded).
You may distribute any DoS you get to do the following:
44
• 1DoS: Receive the item faster, reducing the duration by a step per DoS (for example, a Rare item with 1DoS may be clamed after 1d10 hours, instead of 1 day.
45
• 1DoS: Make the request more subtle, increasing the difficulty of anyone trying to investigate your activities. Note that inquisitorial requests are subtle by default, and this further increases difficulty of Inquiry by -10 per DoS.
46
• 1DoS: If the item is a one-time consumable (such as a grenade, drugs, or a clip), you may dedicate DoS to gain 1 extra item per DoS. This does not apply to parts or materials that are not "used up", such as weapon modifications.
47
• 1DoS: if you score at least 1DoS on a item with ammo, you may dedicate any amount of DoS to receive clips or power packs to power the item, 1 clip/pack per DoS.
48
• 3DoS: if you score at least 3DoS, you may dedicate 3DoS to increase the quality of the item to Good Quality. You must spend another 1 Influence to do so.
49
• 5DoS: if you score at least 5DoS, you may dedicate 5DoS to increase the quality of the item to Best Quality. You must spend another 3 Influence to do so.

FAILURE
52
Neither you nor your contacts could locate or successfuly request the item.
In addition, you may suffer the following penalties (not cummulative):
• 0DoF: If you failed the test, but only just, nothing happens. However, you may choose to receive a single poor quality item at the exchange of doubling the original Influence loss from this test, or if you lost none, to lose 1 (whichever greater).
53
• 1-2DoF: not only you could not get the item, but you attracted attention to it's name, and to the people asking about it. At the discretion of GM, you become more questioned by the locals and gain more unwanted attention.
54
• 3-4DoF: you also got a few of your contacts exposed, or the black marked retreated when hearing rumours of inquisition. Double the original Influence loss from this test. If you lost none, lose 1.
55
• 5DoF: you trashed some of Inquisitorial assets, and exausted some of the levers available to you. Either double the original Influence loss from this test, or lose half of your current influence (rounding down), whichever is greater.
56
• 6+DoF: you are seen to be dabbling with Forbidden knowledge, or simply insulted the high-standing with your presence. You lose 1 Status, and 1 additional status per any further DoF.

SELLING ITEMSREQUISITIONS AND SUBTLIETY
59
You may trade away your items to regain some of the Influence that you had. This will, however, give you less Influence than you started with, with the exception if the item is more rare in the selling location than where you bought it from.
Selling average or more available items does not give any influence. The GM can choose to provide something in return, but usually there is no reward.
Selling scarce or less available items gives Influence equal to their base influence cost. There is no test for this, but it cannot raise your Influence above your Status, and you must be in a location where trade is viable.		Almost all missions lead to someone that you have to hunt down. This is always much easier if the escapee does not know that he is hunted at all. While break-ins and interrogations give this away quite fast, an extended stir-up in town may do the trick just as well.
At GM's discretion, requesting rare items or severely failing the requisition test may alert everyone of your team's presence. Exactly how hard you have to fail or what items you have to ask for is always up to GM.
As a general recommended point to start from, requesting several Rare items or a single Very Rare item may draw attention. Requesting even more rare items will definitely get the talks going.

Spray Spray weapons use large area-effect shots to strike more foes, sacrificing range for wider coverage. Unlike other weapons,they have just one range and, when fired, hit all those in their area of effect. The wielder does not need to test Ballistic Skill; all creatures in the weapon’s path (a cone-shaped area extending in a 30° arc from the firer out to the weapon’s range) must make a Challenging (+0) Agility test or suffer one hit from the weapon. If this test is failed, the target can still attempt to Dodge the attack, as long as he can excape the area of affect.
If the wielder does not possess the appropriate Weapon Training talent, targets gain a +20 bonus on the test; this rises to +30 if the weapon is Heavy and the wielder is not braced. Holding weapon in one hand also gives targets appropriate help for their tests (applies for ambidextrous, TWW, and one-hand-basic-weapon stuff). Spray weapons are not penalised for other conditions, such as small targets or hard visibility conditions.
Cover does not protect from Spray attacks unless it completely conceals a target. Because there is no attack roll, they are always considered to strike the Body location. They jam if the firer rolls a 9 on any damage dice (before any modifiers are applied). Due to the inaccurate nature of their design, they cannot be used to make Called Shot actions.
134
Storm These are purpose-built assault weapons intended to shred targets apart with a flurry of rapid-fire projectiles. Weapons with the Storm quality fire more shots on all modes, and likewise double the number of hits scored on an enemy. Storm weapons fire two shots at single shot, and score an extra hit at 2DoS. When firing on Semi-Fire, they score an extra hit on every DoS. When firing on Full auto, they score 2 extra hits on every DoS. The hits scored cannot exceed the listed firing rate.
135
TearingTearing weapons are vicious devices, often using multitudes of fast-moving jagged teeth or fragmented or explosive ammunition to rip into flesh and bone. These weapons roll one extra die for damage, and the lowest result is discarded.

Toxic (X)Filled with malignant chemicals and fast acting poisons, toxic weapons bring down enemies through virulence and sickness. At the end of his turn, if a character has suffered damage (after Armour and Toughness bonus) since his last turn from a weapon with this quality, he must make a Toughness test with a penalty equal to 10 times the number in parentheses (X) or suffer 1d5 additional damage, plus 1 damage for each degree of failure (of the same type as the first weapon with this quality to damage him that round). For example, a weapon with Toxic (4) imposes a –40 on the tests to resist its effects. If the number in parenthises is negative (ex. -1), then the target gains a bonus on his toughness test. Some weapons or creatures carry additional effects with their toxins or inflict more damage, as indicated in their individual descriptions.
Taking multiple hits from toxic weapons does not cause additional damage. However, for each instance after the first, increase the difficulty of the Toughness test by 10 ( So a 0 test becomes a -10 test, ect.)
137
Twin-LinkedA twin-linked weapon is formed by combining two identical weapons together, which are fired by a single trigger pull (or button press). Twin-linked weapons are built this way in order to increase the chances of scoring a hit on the enemy through the crude expedience of blasting more shots at them. This type of weapon is a popular feature on Imperial armoured vehicles where its bulk and rapid ammunition consumption are non-issues. A Twin-Linked weapon fires twice as many shots, on all modes, as the original weapons that were linked together, and grants its firer a +20 bonus to hit to his Ballistic Skill. The weapon may score one additional hit beyond its normal total as long as the attack succeeds by two or more degrees. Finally, as each weapon has its own ammunition feed, its reload time is doubled.

UnbalancedHeavy, unagile and difficult to ready after an attack, these kinds of weapons permit only limited movement. After the first WS test with this weapon, all WS tests (including Parries) suffer a -10 penalty until the beggining of your next turn. This penalty also applies to Swift/Lightning attack or other multi-hit attacks, even if they are the first attack in a round.

UnreliableCertain weapons misfire more often than normal because they are badly maintained or constructed. An Unreliable weapon suffers a Jam on a roll of 91 or higher, even if fired on Semi or Full Auto.

CRAFTING
147
Skills with the Crafting descriptor are resolved in a different way than most other Skills. You may not know the results of your efforts with only one throw of the dice, and possibly not for extended periods of game time. These are Extended Tests that accumulate results over the course of many Skill Tests, and may take days, weeks or months to complete rather than several Rounds.
The creation of new items can be long, exhausting work. An agri-worker or sculptor alike may toil for months before their efforts come to fruition. Just the design of particularly complex items may take months, to say nothing of their construction. Yet in the end, a patient artisan may be rewarded with a unique item whose appearance, attributes or abilities far exceed those of lesser craftsmanship.
Similarly, you may wish to improve upon an item already in your possession, either by upgrading its functionality or improving its Craftsmanship.
The Acolyte begins the Crafting process by defining the item he wishes to create in defined game terms. The GM uses this information to assign a Difficulty, Duration and Cost to the task. Both the player and GM consider a variety of choices or circumstances that may adjust these assigned factors. Next, the Acolyte makes appropriate Skill Tests after completion of a certain duration of work, and make decisions about how to best use his successes. If all goes well, the Acolyte’s labour will result in a prized item of first-rate workmanship.

STEP 1: DEFINE THE TASK
163
 Define the task. There is an enormous difference between adding a fire selector to an autopistol and creating a custom powersword, or between cooking a quick meal for a group of hungry Acolytes and preparing a fine banquet worthy of Imperial nobles. You must very clearly define your goals in concrete game terms so the GM can accurately determine the Difficulty and process of creating the item. You should think about basic elements, such as the base item, additional or enhanced attributes and abilities, and even reduced performance in some cases. In addition, you should specify any statistics different from those of the base item: weight, special qualities, Armour Points, or anything else appropriate to the task.
It’s the GMs responsibility to ensure that the player has adequately defined the task or item, including all game effects. Should either GM or player miss anything in this step, it is assumed to be the same as a basic item of Common Craftsmanship.
