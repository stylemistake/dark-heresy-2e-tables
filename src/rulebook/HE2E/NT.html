USING INVESTIGATION SKILLS
84
In many cases, clues come as a result of achieving certain objectives in the game. You might travel to another planet to search through ancient xenos ruins, before heading on to a hive-city to find someone who knows a bit about what you found, only to head back to a shrine world to find the corrupted priest who heads up a cult to the Dark Powers. All of these instances can result from knowing where to look, who to talk to, and where to go once you find the information you seek. Sometimes, though, you may need to roll up your sleeves and do some digging to discover the truth. Examples of investigation could be to find out the background to a forbidden planet, to learn more information about a xenos, or to research a particularly famous figure in the history of the Imperium. Luckily, all Investigation skills work the same way.All Investigation tests require a character to have access to an Investigation skill. The Difficulty of the Test depends on its complexity, which also determines the base time it should take to uncover the information sought.
- A success on the Test allows you to reduce the time it takes to uncover the information sought.
- A failed Test indicates no progress gained. Failing by three or more degrees indicates a setback increasing time required. Failing by five or more degrees means that your investigation comes to a complete halt and that the information’s complexity increases by two steps.
- Once you reduce the time to 0 or less, you get the available information on the subject you’re investigating.

FATE POINTS
97
 Fate Points allow you to turn luck to your advantage, hitting with that bolter shot when you would have otherwise missed, or cracking the security code on a door just in time to make a hasty escape. Using these twists of fate, you can take a few more risks, which makes the game faster and far more exciting than would otherwise be the case.
That said, you have a limited pool of Fate Points and whenever you spend a Fate Point, you reduce your pool by one, so choose wisely. Fate Points are restored at the start of the next gaming session. Spending a Fate Point allows a character to do one of the following things:
98
• Re-roll any one Test. If the character has talents that allow them to re-roll a test, they may do that first. Unless otherwise specified, the results of the Fate Point re-roll are final (further FP may not be used). Characters may not reroll non-test rolls, such as damage rolls, phenomena rolls, rolls to choose a mutation, ect.
99
• Count as having rolled a 10 for their Initiative.
100
• Gain a +10 bonus to a test. This must be chosen before the dice are rolled.
101
• Add 1 degree of success to a successful test. This may be chosen after the dice are rolled.
102
• Instantly recover 1d5 Wounds. You can only do this during your own turn, and you can use no more than 1 FP in this manner per session.
103
• Recover from being Stunned.
104
• Remove all levels of Fatigue.
105
Burning Fate Points:
106
 When the situation becomes extremely dire, simply using a Fate Point and getting lucky is not enough. In this case, burning a Fate Point may help. Burning Fate permanently reduces the Acolyte’s Fate threshold by 1, and he immediately loses any Fate points in excess of his new threshold. There are generally two categories of situations where burning a Fate Point will help:
• Cheating death or surpassing immediate difficulties.
- If you are dealt damage that would kill you, instead you survive and set your wounds at 0. You still receive other terminal damage effects, but none of them can kill you. If you are in an engagement, you become unconsciuous until it ends, and enemies do not prioritize you as a target. Note that enemies may still kill you if they win the engagement and find out that you survived afterwards. If you regain consciousness and attempt to rejoin the battle, you no longer benefit from reduced priority as a target.
• Slow escape to a safe location.
- If you must overcome extended difficulties that would lead to your demise, such as unlocking a cell door before an execution, escaping a collapsing starship, or crawling from a battlefield to a place the enemy will not find you. This use can be intercepted by enemies, and it will not immediately take you away to safety from an engagement, if you are fighting in one.

Note that burning a single Fate Point may not be enough to save you. Surviving a lethal bullet is not good enough if you remain in the battlefield to be discovered by enemies. Escaping a crashing ship in the last drop-pod will not save you if you bleed to death. Similarly, finding a way to unlock your cell is nothing when you are still in a building filled with guards that you have to fight.
In that case, you may have to burn 2 Fate Points for the same situation. Crawling from battlefield can supplement surviving a lethal bullet. Surviving bleeding can supplement escape in a pod. Avoiding essential recognition by guards can supplement prison escape. In any case, 2 Fate Point burn saves you from any situation and circumstance, and brings you back to a safe location from where it must be possible to safely retreat.
GM should keep in mind that burning Fate Points is very expensive, and players have valid expectations to get a result. Thus, it should be very clear what the Fate Point burn saves the character from. Also, the save should be significant; it does not count that the character has escaped engagement if he runs into more enemies after the next corner.
107
 It is up to the GM and the player to work out exactly how the character survives the incident. In the case of an injury sustained in combat, perhaps a holy icon worn on the Acolyte’s breast deflects a blow, or a bullet passes cleanly through his chest, miraculously missing organs. Other situations, such as a Warp drive implosion, or being on the surface of a world during Exterminatus, require a greater degree of creativity to explain, and should seldom be without some consequence.
108
 If the GM has seen the players back themselves into a corner and they act suicidal or can't be bothered to role-play to get themselves out of a mess, then some character deaths or even a TPK (Total Party Wipe) is entirely deserving. Burning fate points should never be a way for the players to bypass a plot element and give a big FU to the GM and the campaign in general.

ABPer minPer hourPer day A standard day's march is 10 hours long.
There’s nothing stopping characters from pushing themselves beyond the standard. However, after 10 hours, for every additional hour that he starts marching the character must test Toughness or take 1 Fatigue. Characters may safely push themselves for a number of extra hours equal to their Toughness Bonus. HURRYING IN NARRATIVE TIME
125
 Characters can run or hurry during Narrative Time, but doing so is tiring. When hurrying, a character triples his rate of movement, but each hour of sustained running requires the character to Test Toughness, with a cumulative –10 penalty per hour after the first, to sustain the pace. On a failed Test, the character takes 1 level of Fatigue, but he can continue hurrying.
Characters moving at this brisk pace are focused on running and watching their steps, so they take a –20 penalty to all Perception Tests.
Characters can Hurry when Forced marching. In this case, they take Toughness test twice, one for each of these conditions.
If the character has Hurried at least a bit, he removes Fatigue by resting at half speed. This debuff lasts until the character no longer has any Fatigue.

The movement rates for characters described on Movement (Metres/Round) table assume a relatively clear battlefield. There may be a few obstacles, puddles or wisps of fog, but characters can more or less, move at their standard rates. There are, however, some circumstances that reduce a character’s speed. These can include rubble-strewn hallways, deep snow, dense fog, thick underbrush and a variety of other conditions that make it tough to navigate. In such environments, a character’s movement is halved. If a character charges or runs, he must succeed on an Agility Test or fall prone. The difficulty of this Test depends on the terrain.
